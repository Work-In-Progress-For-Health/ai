## What the data shows

Seventeen deployments over about 22 months, but the story is really about two distinct eras separated by a clear discontinuity.

**Era 1 (Apr 2023 – May 2024):** Ten deployments across ~13 months. The first nine follow a roughly bi-weekly to monthly cadence through mid-2023, then things slow dramatically. After `1.8.0` on Dec 8, 2023, there's a nearly six-month gap before `1.10.1` appears in late May 2024. Note that `1.9.x` is missing entirely from the list — it was either skipped, rolled back, or deployed somewhere this data doesn't capture.

**Era 2 (Aug 2024 – Feb 2025):** Seven deployments across ~6 months, cadence ranging from a week to six weeks, averaging roughly every three weeks. The version jump from `1.10.1` straight to `2.4.48` is the giveaway — this isn't the same codebase on a natural evolution. Either the product was re-platformed, a major rewrite was merged, the team adopted an upstream dependency's versioning (the `2.4.x` pattern with high patch numbers is very Apache httpd / nginx-flavored), or the team switched to tracking a vendored component's releases.

## Mapping to DORA tiers

Using the standard thresholds: Era 1's front half (Apr–Aug 2023) is roughly *high* performer territory — weekly-ish deploys. Era 1's tail and the handoff to Era 2 drops into *medium* (monthly). Era 2 is solidly *medium* — every few weeks. Overall trajectory: started strong, stalled for six months, restarted at a less frequent but more consistent pace.

But these tiers only mean something if deployment frequency is actually the right lens for this system. Worth pausing on that before recommending anything.

## The things that stand out

The **six-month gap from Dec 2023 to May 2024** is the most interesting signal. Holidays explain the first few weeks. Everything after that needs a different explanation — a hard refactor, a failed release, team turnover, a pivot, a compliance freeze, an incident response consuming cycles. This is where I'd dig first.

The **missing 1.9.x** series matters. Skipped version numbers usually mean either a release was cut and pulled, an internal-only version existed, or someone renumbered to signal significance. Combined with the long gap, it smells like a release that didn't go well.

The **jump to 2.4.48** is not organic. Nobody naturally arrives at patch 48 on their first 2.x release. The most likely explanations are (a) the version now tracks an upstream/vendored project, (b) there was a rebase onto a fork of something, or (c) internal builds were being numbered continuously and only now started being published externally. Each has different implications — (a) means "deployment" may now include pulling in upstream changes the team didn't write, which isn't really what DORA is measuring.

Era 2's cadence is **more regular but less frequent** than early Era 1. Bi-weekly-ish with low variance. That often indicates a team that's adopted a sprint/train release model after previously deploying ad-hoc. Whether that's progress depends on what they traded away.

## Questions to ask

- What happened between December 2023 and May 2024? This is the single most important question.
- What do the version jumps (skipped 1.9, jump to 2.4.48) represent? Re-platform? Vendored dependency? Renumbering?
- Is a "deployment" here a production push, a release tag, a published artifact, or a customer-visible update? Era 2's pattern in particular could be artifact publishing rather than deploys.
- Are the other three DORA metrics tracked — lead time, change failure rate, MTTR? Deploy frequency in isolation can mislead. A team that went from weekly-with-rollbacks to tri-weekly-with-none is improving, not regressing.
- Is this a SaaS service, a library, a shipped product, or a vendored fork? DORA's thresholds assume the first.
- Were there team changes, re-orgs, or leadership changes around late 2023?
- Does the team consider the Era 2 cadence deliberate, or is it the pace they ended up at?

## Recommendations — held lightly

If this is a SaaS service and the team wants to get back to Era 1's early cadence: the usual levers apply (trunk-based development, feature flags decoupling deploy from release, smaller PRs, investment in CI speed and test reliability). But I wouldn't prescribe those yet. The gap in early 2024 likely reflects something structural — technical debt, a failed launch, attrition, scope change — and faster deploys won't fix whatever that was.

If this is a library or vendored component, Era 2's cadence is probably fine and trying to "fix" it would be solving the wrong problem.

The more useful framing for the team itself: Era 2 looks like a team that has rebuilt a release discipline after something went wrong. The next question isn't "can you go faster" but "is the current pace sustainable, and what would it take to safely shorten the interval by one step" — from ~3 weeks to ~2, not from ~3 weeks back to every-few-days.

## The honest read

Deployment frequency is telling you *something happened* around the turn of 2024, and the team recovered with a slower but steadier rhythm. That's valuable signal. It is not, by itself, a performance verdict. Pair it with change failure rate and lead time before drawing conclusions, and find out what the version jumps actually represent before comparing to DORA benchmarks.
